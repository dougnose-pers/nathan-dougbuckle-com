05 / The anchor

The Workshop.
Two days of design thinking. Four weeks of follow-on. Delivered in one.

A senior Katapult designer simulates a full design workshop using an orchestrated stack of AI and industry-leading research tools. The client spends ten minutes on a portal; Katapult does the rest in a week. Output worth $25K, priced at $4K. One in four clients then commissions a real project.

This is the product that puts Katapult ahead of the industry. Not because it's a clever AI workflow — there are plenty of those — but because of what Katapult orchestrates under one roof, and what it deliberately chooses to charge for it.

The offer, in one line

$4K for $25K of work.
The gift that becomes the project.

What used to take a two-day in-person design workshop plus four weeks of follow-on design work — roughly an $80K engagement under the old model — Katapult now delivers in a single week, for four thousand dollars, led by a senior designer working with an orchestrated stack of AI capabilities and industry-leading research tools.

The client doesn't get a sales pitch. They get the actual work — the same depth of research, trend analysis, problem framing, customer voice, concept exploration, sketches, industry positioning, and senior critique they'd have got in the old model. Just compressed, and absurdly under-priced relative to its value.

They walk away knowing exactly what Katapult is capable of, because they just experienced it. Roughly one in four comes back and commissions a real project. Average size: eighty thousand dollars. The workshop pays for itself; the project work is the prize.

How it actually runs

Ten minutes from the client. One week from Katapult.

Client side, day one. The client and two or three members of their team each spend ten minutes on a Katapult portal. They answer structured questions about their product, their customer, their category, the strategic question keeping them up at night. That's the entire client commitment of time.

Katapult side, the week that follows. A senior Katapult designer leads a simulated workshop. The AI stack runs the synthesis at speed — research, macro trends, simulated customer voice, problem framing, concept divergence, sketches, ideation, industry analysis, business positioning. Six to eight times across the week, the senior intervenes — about thirty minutes each — to redirect, sharpen, choose, reframe, and shape what the AI is producing. Three to four hours of senior judgement, layered into a week of orchestrated AI work.

Day seven. The client receives a polished design discovery report. It captures everything a traditional two-day workshop plus four weeks of design follow-on would have produced. Same depth. Same quality of thinking. Same kind of decisions enabled.

What's actually under the hood

Best of the industry, orchestrated by Katapult.

The four-thousand-dollar price point is only sustainable because Katapult orchestrates a stack of capabilities most agencies can't pull off in one engagement. The client experiences a single polished output. What they don't see is what's running underneath.

The capabilities Katapult brings to bear

Each workshop draws on:

  • Macro and global trend intelligence — Katapult pays the subscriptions a single client could never justify (Future Laboratory-tier sources) and amortises them across every workshop run. The client gets a 3–5 year horizon on their category, woven into the output, without ever needing to know where it came from.
  • Simulated customer voice — purpose-built AI personas, run to brief, producing real qualitative signal in days rather than weeks of recruitment. Best-in-class research capability brought in behind the scenes, delivered as part of the Katapult deliverable.
  • Cross-category pattern recognition — twenty years of Katapult project history, structured for retrieval. The wellness-hardware client gets the kitchen-appliance pattern they'd otherwise never see. The premium-product founder gets the sport-tech lesson that maps perfectly.
  • Manufacturing reality, in the room — Katapult's curated supplier knowledge, certification timelines, materials cost benchmarks. The "is this actually buildable?" question gets answered in the workshop, not three months later.
  • AI-augmented synthesis — the speed engine. Concept divergence, sketch generation, deck assembly, written narrative. AI does the hours of work in minutes.

Put together, this stack is what makes a four-thousand-dollar workshop sustainable while delivering twenty-five thousand dollars of value. No single client could subscribe to all of it; Katapult assembles it once and runs it across many workshops. This is the secret sauce.

The senior layer — the irreplaceable bit

Capabilities don't critique themselves. Seniors do.

The orchestrated stack above is necessary but not sufficient. The thing that makes a Katapult workshop better than the same stack run by anyone else is the senior in the room — a Katapult designer who intervenes at every stage gate, redirects what the AI got wrong, names what the AI got right, and shapes what the client takes home.

Ten things even the best AI stack still can't do without senior judgement:

  1. Knowing what to ask. AI generates infinite answers to wrong questions.
  2. Knowing when something is bullshit. Distinguishing the good answer from the merely plausible one.
  3. Connecting cross-category patterns. A wellness hardware client only knows wellness hardware; the senior has seen ten categories.
  4. Reading which way the room is leaning. The nervous laugh, the disagreement between CEO and head of product.
  5. Sequencing the work. AI generates everything at once. Humans know what matters first.
  6. Reaching for the right reference. Apple is obvious. Bellroy is specific to context.
  7. Reframing the brief. Client thinks they're building a wallet; should be building a wallet-shaped membership.
  8. Forcing convergence. AI defaults to more options. Humans know when to stop.
  9. Knowing what actually ships. Twenty years of production reality.
  10. The earned authority to say "this won't work." Trust that AI cannot have.

The senior is the principal. The stack is the instrument. Other agencies will build similar stacks; few have Katapult's senior bench to layer on top.

Why this is the strategic anchor

Everything else in the roadmap attaches here.

The workshop is not a revenue product. It's a trust-builder and a value-demonstrator, deliberately under-priced so the only honest response from a serious client is *"how do we work together properly?"* That's why every other idea in this reading attaches to it.

Slingshot uses the workshop methodology as the foundation of its productised tiers. The partner company doesn't reinvent it — it inherits it.

The "Hardware is the new moat" LinkedIn series closes into the workshop. The final piece's call-to-action is "book a Katapult workshop." Without the workshop, the series has nowhere to land readers.

The eight productised tools attach to workshops on the way in and to the projects they convert into on the way out. Brand identity, pitch deck, factory onboarding pack — these become natural follow-ons to a workshop that landed well.

Katapult is already a leading agency. This product is what puts it ahead of the industry: not the AI, not the productisation, not even the partner stack — but the deliberate choice to give clients twenty-five thousand dollars of value for four, and let the trust do the rest.
