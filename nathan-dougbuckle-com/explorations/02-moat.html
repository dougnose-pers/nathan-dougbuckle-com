02 / The series

Hardware is the new moat.

Four short pieces published as you're ready. Builds Katapult's public position as AI eats the digital moats and the physical, the audience, and the built-over-time become the new defensibility.

This idea is the cheapest of the five to ship and possibly the highest-leverage. A short, well-argued series of pieces under your name, properly positioned, becomes a strategic asset Katapult can compound on for years.

Where we landed

Four pieces. Your voice. Drop when ready.

Author: you, alone. Distribution: LinkedIn and the Katapult blog (blog hosts the canonical version, LinkedIn drives reach). Length: ~600 words each, skimmable in 90 seconds. Cadence: drop when each one feels ready — no rigid schedule. Doug's role on this idea is framing only — develop the thesis, structure, hooks, and examples. You write.

The competitive landscape isn't empty. Patrick Collison, Ben Thompson, a16z's "American Dynamism" all talk about versions of "physical is back." What Katapult uniquely owns is the practitioner's lens — *"here's what it actually looks like in the studio when a founder tries to build a hardware moat. Here's where it usually breaks."* Specific, grounded, designerly. That cuts through.

The four pieces

An arc, not a list.

Each piece earns the next read. Pieces 1–3 build the argument. Piece 4 is the only one that pitches Katapult — earned by what came before.

Piece 01
Hardware is the new moat.

"In the last twelve months I've watched three founders tear up their digital roadmaps. Not because they failed — because their competitors' AI just made the digital piece trivial."

Piece 02
Your "AI moat" isn't a moat.

"Last month a founder pitched me their 'AI-powered' platform. Spent forty minutes explaining how clever the AI was. I asked what their competitor would have to do to copy it. He didn't have an answer."

Piece 03
What hardware moats actually look like.

"Bellroy is twelve years old. They sell something every reader already owns — a wallet. Their gross margin is north of sixty per cent. Why? It's not the design. It's not the brand. It's something else."

Piece 04
How to start building one this quarter.

"If you've read the previous three pieces nodding, you probably have one question: where do I start? Here's what I tell people."

Why it was interesting

Three reasons this earns the time it takes.

First — it's true. AI has genuinely collapsed cost-to-build for software, apps, content, brand systems, even competent product mockups. Things that used to be moats — proprietary code, polished UX, marketing production capacity — are eroding fast. What's left really is physical, audience, distribution, real-world data, reputation built over time. The argument doesn't have to be exaggerated to land.

Second — it positions Katapult uniquely. Most AI-first design agencies (Clay, LCA, Punchcut, Lazarev) play in UX/UI. Almost nobody owns the *physical* product design angle of the AI-rearranging-defensibility conversation. There's space to be the named voice on this.

Third — it feeds the funnel. Pieces close into the discovery workshop kickoff and (once it's live) Slingshot. A founder reads piece 04, recognises themselves, books a workshop. Long-term content compounding into qualified leads.

Voice notes

First-person, present-tense. Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph. Specific over general — always reach for a brand name, a number, an anecdote. Strong opinion, no swagger. No hedging openers ("in this article we'll explore..."). Land the point in sentence one. Close with a question or a move, not a summary.
