The one we walked away from.

A website tool where founders pitch their idea and get a Katapult-style validation report. Considered, reframed, and ultimately set aside. Not a defeat — discipline.

The idea, in plain language

A tool on the website. An instant research report as the entry point to Katapult.

The proposition: a tool on katapult.com.au where a prospective client could submit their product concept and quickly receive a Katapult-style research report. The first page available immediately on submission, the deeper analysis walked through with the sales team. Half lead magnet, half value demonstrator — a way for a serious operator to experience Katapult's thinking before ever booking a meeting.

A clean motion on paper. Worth working through carefully.

Where we landed

Parked. For honest reasons.

Two structural problems didn't survive scrutiny:

Founders most worth attracting are least willing to share IP into a web form. Serious operators are protective of their product thinking — especially now, as AI tools make every piece of input easier to replicate. A tool that asks for IP filters toward enthusiastic inventors and away from the enterprise prospects Katapult is built to serve.

The pattern recognition that would have powered the tool belongs somewhere else. Katapult's edge — twenty years of cross-category pattern recognition — is more powerful when it's demonstrated in a workshop or an essay than when it's requested in exchange for filling out a form.

The right move was to park it cleanly and put Katapult's pattern-recognition edge on surfaces that don't ask the client to surrender anything first.

"The founders most worth attracting are exactly the ones least willing to type their idea into a stranger's form. It's not a UX problem you fix with reassuring copy."
— The clearest reason the idea didn't survive
What this tells us about everything else

Lead generation that asks for IP filters the wrong way.

Two principles for how Katapult attracts the clients it wants, drawn from this exploration:

Demonstrate the recognition, don't request it. Show Katapult's category insight in a published piece, in a workshop output a client has already paid for, in a case study about work that shipped. Don't ask a stranger to type their idea into a form for an unproven payoff.

Make Katapult's surfaces feel like the work, not like the sales. A founder reading a sharp piece on hardware moats, or experiencing a Katapult workshop, gets the value before any commitment is made. That's what the validation tool was trying to do — but it asked for trust before earning it.

This principle now lives inside two of the other ideas in this reading. The workshop earns trust by delivering $25K of value for $4K. The LinkedIn series earns it by being publicly visible thinking. Together they do what the validation tool was trying to do, but cleanly.
