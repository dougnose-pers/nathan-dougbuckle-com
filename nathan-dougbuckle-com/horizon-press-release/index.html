Six months of Horizon. Six clients in. Here's what comes next.

In May, Katapult took six seats off the shelf and called it a quarterly intelligence subscription. Twelve dispatches and two project handoffs later, Horizon is doing exactly what we hoped — and a few things we didn't see coming.

When we launched Horizon in May 2026, the premise was simple. Most of our clients don't need a six-figure foresight retainer — and most foresight retainers don't deliver the kind of pointed, opinionated, design-led intelligence that actually changes a roadmap. We thought there was a gap for something in between: senior-written dispatches, four times a year, six seats only, priced so a head of marketing or a head of strategy could approve it without going to a steering committee.

We weren't sure who would sign up. We had a hunch it would be category leaders trying to stay category leaders — the people most exposed to "we knew that was coming but didn't move fast enough." It turned out to be a slightly different profile: half the seats went to incumbents, half went to ambitious challengers who'd been quietly stockpiling capital and were now looking for places to deploy it. Both groups wanted the same thing — a signal layer they could trust, in a format their team would actually read.

What two cycles taught us

The first thing that surprised us is how much the dispatches got passed around. We'd built Horizon as a service to a single subscriber. By dispatch three, the average report was being forwarded to between four and seven people inside the client org — exec teams, board members, NPD leads, one CFO. The conversations that came out of those forwards became the second surprise: four of our six subscribers commissioned follow-on project work with Katapult inside the first two quarters. None of them were planning to. Each one read a dispatch and saw something they wanted to act on.

The third surprise was strategic. We thought Horizon was a revenue product. It turned out to also be a positioning product. Two of the most interesting pitches we won this year — both six-figure design engagements, both in categories Katapult hadn't worked in before — came from prospects who'd read a forwarded Horizon dispatch and asked who wrote it.

Where we go to next

We've made a small number of decisions for the next six months, and we're sharing them here so subscribers, prospects, and our own team are working from the same map.

01 — Hold the line on six

The roster stays at six

We were tempted to expand to nine after the first cycle. We're not. Six is what lets senior writers keep their hands on the work, and what lets us promise no two subscribers ever sit in the same category. Scarcity isn't a marketing posture here — it's the product.

02 — Launch Horizon: Studio

A second tier, for design teams

The dispatches kept getting forwarded into design and innovation teams who wanted to build from them. From Q1 2027 we'll offer a lighter Studio tier — quarterly dispatches plus two sketch sessions and a half-day workshop, capped at twelve clients. Same scarcity logic, different price point.

03 — Open Sydney + Auckland seats

Two cities, two senior leads

The first six seats were Melbourne-led. From the next cycle we're opening two seats run out of Sydney and a single seat run out of Auckland, each with a named lead. Same format, same standard. Wider net for inbound, and a way to test the model in a second market without diluting the first.

Looking ahead

We built Horizon to be the dispatch that gets forwarded.

Six months in, the model holds. Senior writers, not analysts. Pointed views, not landscape scans. A roster small enough that every dispatch can be specific to a subscriber's category — and a price small enough that the question of whether to renew is never a question.

The next six months are about discipline. Hold the roster at six. Launch Studio carefully. Open Sydney and Auckland with the same standards as Melbourne. Keep the dispatches the kind of thing a CEO forwards to a board chair.

To the six subscribers who took a seat without a track record to point at: thank you. To the design and strategy leads inside those organisations who turned a forwarded PDF into a conversation that changed direction: that's the part we're most proud of. And to the team at Katapult — Trav, the senior writers, the design crew — who turned what was a thought experiment in March into a recurring product line by November: that part was the hardest, and you made it look easy.
