What's happening in the market

The bottom of our market is about to be claimed.

The shift

AI has just compressed concept design work from 60 hours to 25 hours (we saw it ourselves on the WA ground-effect project). The economics of the $5K–$30K project segment have changed. A productised AI-first competitor in this space is inevitable in the next 12–18 months.

The gap

Designjoy is doing $1.3M ARR with one operator in graphic/web. AI-first UX agencies like Clay and LCA own the premium digital end. Nobody owns AI-first, productised, sub-$30K physical product design. The gap is real and uncontested.

What we leave on the table

Every month we turn away or scare off founders whose budgets sit at $5K–$30K. They build with someone worse, or they don't build at all. A meaningful slice of those conversations could land in a productised offer that meets them where they are.

Why now

The AI workflow exists and works. The market hasn't named a player here yet. Nathan raised the "Katapult Ventures" framing himself two weeks ago. Window is open, motivation is fresh, conviction is high. The action moment is now.

Introducing

Slingshot — a partner company to Katapult.

Slingshot is a separate-brand, AI-first product design venture. Same studio family, different lane. Same calibre of judgement, smaller scope of engagement. The line we want people to hear is simple:

"Can't afford Katapult? Talk to our partner company Slingshot."

It captures the market segment Katapult cannot profitably serve today, runs lean with one operator and the AI harness as their leverage, uses Katapult's senior team as a stage-gate critique on every job, and hands clients up to Katapult main when their projects outgrow Slingshot's $28K ceiling — with a referral fee that keeps the incentives clean.

How it works, in plain language

Small company, big backing.
Built so everyone wins together.

The detailed structure follows. But the seven principles below hold the whole thing — if a stakeholder can hold these in their head, they understand Slingshot.

  1. Nathan keeps control. Katapult is the largest shareholder and holds a brand-protective veto on anything that touches the parent name.
  2. A key person gets the chance to grow a company — internal first if the right candidate is already inside Katapult, external if not.
  3. They take meaningful equity in the success of the new venture.
  4. They draw a fallback salary at roughly 30–40% of what they'd otherwise be on. Enough to live; low enough to be entrepreneurial.
  5. The business pays the rest of their salary as revenue comes in — distributions scale with profitability.
  6. Together everyone grows it — and their stake in it — over time.
  7. 10% is reserved for advisors who help along the way (Doug, and potentially others as needed).

The candidate — internal first

The right person is almost certainly already inside Katapult. Senior, hungry for a step-up, AI-curious. They take the operator role with the verbal commitment from Nathan that if it's not working at 18 months, they return to their existing position with no loss of seniority. That fallback is the single most important hiring lever — it lets us offer a step-up role to someone good without asking them to bet their career.

The backstop

Katapult senior provides 2–4 hours per Slingshot engagement on stage-gate critique. Charged to Slingshot at internal rate, capped at $1,500 per engagement. That's the moat — AI does the speed, our senior team does the judgement that prevents the AI's competent-but-generic output from becoming Slingshot's brand.

What could go wrong

The risks we're naming up front.

No hiding the obvious. Each risk with the realistic likelihood and the specific mitigation we've built into the model.

Can't find the right internal candidate
Medium
Decide on external search at end of month 1. Don't drag it out. Have a clear "stop and reassess" trigger.
Brand bleed — Slingshot work reflects on Katapult
Low–Med
Senior stage-gate on every job. Separate visual brand. Katapult's golden-share veto on brand use.
Channel conflict with Katapult main clients
Medium
Hard rules in operating agreement (any Katapult client of last 36 months is off-limits). Referral-fee aligns incentives.
Content / funnel ramp slow (no TikTok traction)
High
Don't depend on TikTok alone. LinkedIn + direct outreach + Nathan/Doug network for first 12 months. Assume slow content compounding.
AI tooling commoditises faster than expected
Medium
The moat is the workflow design + senior critique, not the AI itself. Tools are replaceable; judgement isn't.
Operator burnout in year 1
Medium
Realistic salary expectations set upfront. Monthly board check-ins on workload. The 18-month fallback is the safety valve.
Revenue ramp slower than projected
Medium
$85K capital + revenue ramping from Q2 gives 12 months runway even on conservative projections.
The decision in front of us

What we're asking the Katapult team to commit to.

  1. 01 In-principle agreement to launch Slingshot as a partner company to Katapult, with the structure described in this document.
  2. 02 Identification of a candidate — ideally an internal Katapult senior who'd take the operator role, with the verbal 18-month fallback to their current position.
  3. 03 Capital commitment of $85,000 from Katapult Pty Ltd to Slingshot Pty Ltd as equity buy-in for the 45% stake.
  4. 04 Brand licence agreement allowing Slingshot to reference Katapult as its partner company in marketing.
  5. 05 Operational commitment for senior Katapult time on stage-gate critiques (2–4 hours per engagement, paid back at internal rate).
What we're NOT asking for
  • Significant ongoing time from Nathan personally — target 1–2 days/month for first six months, then half a day/month
  • Free senior staff time — every hour is paid back at internal rate
  • Any client disruption to Katapult main — explicit off-limits rules
  • Any change to Katapult's existing brand, positioning, or service model
Decision timeline requested: 4 weeks from this document. Slow enough for the team to consult properly. Fast enough to maintain momentum.

— Closing

The case for Slingshot is not that it changes what Katapult is. The case is that it lets Katapult keep being exactly what Katapult is — senior, considered, premium, judgement-led — while a separate, leaner, faster-moving entity captures the part of the market we currently can't profitably serve.

If the experiment works, Katapult gains a new income line, a new lead-gen channel, and a defensive position against the AI-first competitor that's coming anyway. If it doesn't, $85K is the cost of finding out, and the brand walks away unmarked.

The window for this is open now because we have the AI workflow, we have the conviction, and we have — almost certainly — the right person already inside the building. The question is whether we move while those three things are aligned.
